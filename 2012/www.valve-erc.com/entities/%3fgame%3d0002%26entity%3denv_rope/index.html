env_rope
Opposing Force entity

description

    The entity creates a climbable rope.

usage

    Ropes and wires work like ladders from the original Half-Life. If you approach a rope or wire and touch it you attach to it. To detach, hit the jump key or climb off the end of the rope. Look in the direction which you wish to move and press forward or backward to move up and down the rope. You can strafe left and right on the rope also, this swings in those respective directions. To swing, face in the direction you wish to swing and use the movement keys. The ropes work like real life, the lower you are on the rope, the greater the swing distance.

    Notes: Ropes and Electrified wires
    Ropes and electrified wires are very powerful entities and are simple to implement, but there are some potential problems that they pose for level designers from a technical standpoint.

    These entities share many of the same characteristics since electrified wires were derived from the rope code.

    While a rope is placed like any normal point entity, the center of the entity in World Craft when placed should be at the intersection of the brush face of the area (usually a ceiling brush) of where it shall hang. If the rope behaves oddly such as being bunched up, or colliding oddly with the world, there could be two problems.

    1. The rope entity that was placed may be inside world geometry, try moving the env_rope entity 1 unit away from the surface from which it is hanging.
    2. The rope is too long and is colliding with world geometry (e.g. hitting the ground).


    Ropes and wires use more entities from a game engine standpoint then just the one entity you see placed in World Craft. So when checking the number of entities using World Craft listings or third party tools, ropes and wires have to be treated as special cases. This is important because placing a large number of ropes (or one really long rope with a large number of segments) with many, many segments will take up a huge amount of entities. Too many entities will of course take up all the available edicts, and the level will either not load, or if other entities are created or introduced, the game will crash. A good way to test how close a level is to hitting its maximum edicts is hitting impulse 101 multiple times.

    For those that wish too know how many entities a rope or wire takes here is a simple equation:

      Number of entities = (Number of segments x 2) + 1

    For example, a 16 segment rope would take up 33 entities. You can see how a few ropes can easily exceed the game entity limits.

    This is a rough estimate, because for wires, there will be the added effects such as sparks, beams, etc…

    Another design consideration as mentioned above, is that the bottom of the ropes or wires should be up off the ground. This is due to collision problems.

    Ropes SHOULD NOT BE USED IN MULTIPLAYER MAPS. For many reasons, ropes should not be used in multiplayer. The number of entities they create, the amount of network information they transmit, who gets rope control with multiple players on the ropes, etc… Ropes and wires will be disabled most likely in future patches to Op For.

    To prevent collision with the world, and the problems associated with this occurrence, the ropes and wires have some safeguards. When a player is attached to the rope, the model origin attaches to the rope segment origin. This is why when a player is on the rope, and they go to the last rope segment, they fall off when it "appears" that they were not close to the end. This is due to the origin to origin attachment and the OFFSET of the player view from the origin of the model. Hence an optical illusion is created. Also, the player cannot climb up the top 3 segments, this is to prevent the player from effectively climbing into the ceiling or world geometry.

    Tips:

    1. Use the different rope models wisely. They will enable a level designer to make long ropes that minimize entities. Rope models exist at 16, 24, and 32 units in length. (rope16.mdl, rope24.mdl, rope32.mdl)

      Wires have the added benefit of having a separate model for the end, so sizes can be combined for aesthetics. Wires also have 3 different colors to choose from (black, blue, red) and have another length available, and 8 unit segment.

    2. Any model can be used, not just rope or wire models. This makes for some funny ropes, a chain of Otis models that are climbable!
