HLFort
by Dale Broadbent
review by Dave Johnston

INFORMATION
gametype: TFC CTF
number of players: 12-16
download: hlfort.zip

As soon as you enter this map you notice one thing – it’s dark. Then you notice a second thing – the darkness is fading. Within seconds you are presented with another 2fort-inspired creation. Same objective, similar layout, similar size.

Visually, the map differs a lot from 2fort. It actually bears more resemblance to the original 2fort4 map from QWTF – notice the flaming torches and questionable texture choices. It is clear the author has tried to apply a new feel to the map – and we must credit him for being so bold – however in my eyes it falls flat on its face. While most of the time the texturing does relate to the geometry it’s applied to (such as rock textures in a traditionally rocky area), there are various occasions where different textures would have worked better. Primarily a lack of textual integrity is the source of this – keeping to a more rigid theme, and continuing this theme across the map, the author could have created a more consistent look. As it stands, the map feels unpolished and incomplete.

Geometry-wise, there is also some question. It is clear the author knows how to use the tools, and understands what the engine is capable of. Yet I believe there is still some way to go in the way of design-sense. The architecture while original, also echoes lack of experience. It is uninspiring and awkward. To focus on one issue, we can look at the central area (with the famous 2fort bridge). The bridge is present, but created solely from transparent grating – by itself it looks odd and out-of place. It needs more work to look more sensible, and perhaps more convincing.

To keep the r_speeds low, attempts have been made to make some of the architecture func_wall’s. Unfortunately it seems how to use this method to reduce the polycount has been either misinterpreted or misunderstood. I noticed that huge sections of geometry had been made entities, including large sections of rock and structural supports. Even a large expanse of grass in the central area was an entity to recreate the effect of being in the grass. In a game such as TFC it would have been better left solid. In some cases brushes had been made entities which would have worked better as world brushes and blocked VIS – meaning lower polycounts. On other occasions some of these brushes would have been better left as world geometry – many could have saved polys by using the single-unit spacing ‘trick’, while also keeping the entity traffic low, as well as adding to the visuals by casting shadows. One of the side-effects of making structures entities appear from the flag rooms – arches elsewhere can be seen floating in the sky.

The map does feature a lot of new media however, mostly taken from Gunman Chronicles. What you will notice is the new sky, sounds, sprites, and models that have also been pulled from the game, and indeed some have been created specifically for this map. Among the items, there are walltorches (which look much better than the map’s own torch geometry) and new backpacks. There is also a door, included as a result of one of the changes to the 2fort base – destructable scenery allowing for another entrance.

Instead of the single water route in 2forts, hlfort has two routes in its place, minus the water, which rests at the very base of the map and doesn’t assist gameplay. One route heads straight underneath the bridge, each end terminating just a door away from the basement – allowing for each access to either team’s flag if the door has been det-packed. However, this door can also be replaced by a well-equipped engineer. The second route is much lower than the first, giving traditional-2fort-style access.

Due to the many differences in scale, size and design of the map, it is impossible to assume it plays just like 2fort does. It doesn’t. hlfort is far larger, and potentially easier to defend. Particularly, conflict arises between the direct route from each flag room – once the doors are blown, an efficient route is formed, providing quick access for both getting the enemy flag and returning to base quickly. Of course it is in both teams interest to keep their own door in place, but this could be seen as hassle considering how easy it is for the enemy team to remove it. Such a direct route is always a concern for balance.

Defending the flag, and indeed even grabbing it shares more similarities to QWTF’s 2fort4 than 2fort5 or TFC’s 2fort. Leading to the flag room is a single long corridor, barely enough to fit two people going both ways. Given the L-bend at one end, a SG is very well suited for defence, as is a HWGuy. Any attackers could be easily trapped in the corner. However a sly scout could, given the chance, concussion-grenade jump over any defence and back over again, due to the height of the ceiling in the hallway. There is one problem I have with the flag room itself – the grating. Around the flag there is a very small area that a player can easily fall into, and die in the liquid beneath. While this shouldn’t be a problem to most, players must be cautious – while one can simply run over the gap (the preferred method), on occasion during testing I fell in. Jumping onto the platform seems to be the only sure-fire way of not falling in, adding another hurdle.

Unfortunately the layout of the map fails to encourage teamplay. The multitude of routes means players will often find themselves going in alone, and most battles revolve around just a few people at a time at the most. It is clear the changes and additions are added to add a new depth to the 2fort base, but most seem ill-founded and hinder the map instead. Some routes are far too long (especially compared to the original) – the ‘spiral’ is such a long journey that any enemy returning that way with the flag is unlikely to get past the opposition. While the added routes do give them an alternative, these also tip the balance the other way and give the attacker a huge advantage due to the very small distance from their base to the flag. A compromise would improve this area of the map.

FINAL WORD
A good attempt at a 2fort inspired map demonstrating the authors excellent ability and knowledge of the editor. Unfortunately there is still some way to go in understanding the game engine. Gameplay-wise, it lacks the polish of 2fort and doesn’t encourage teamplay to the same extent, essentially due to the size and layout of the huge bases. Aesthetically, it shows gallant attempts to be original, but perhaps a more consistent theme would have helped create a more convincing environment. With more polish, work and some reduction in size and routes, plus some simplification, it could become a far more inviting experience.

RATING: 4.0
visual: 4
ambient: 3
gameplay: 5
All ratings out of 10.0
